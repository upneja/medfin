## 1. Types of Medical School Loans

### Federal Direct Unsubsidized Loans
- **Interest Rate (2025-26):** 7.94% fixed (down from 8.08% in 2024-25)
- **Annual Limit:** $20,500 for graduate/professional students
- **Aggregate Limit:** $138,500 (including undergraduate borrowing), though medical students historically qualified for up to $224,000
- **Key Feature:** Interest accrues from the date of disbursement (not subsidized), including while in school
- **Origination Fee:** 1.057% for loans disbursed before October 1, 2026

### Federal Direct Grad PLUS Loans
- **Interest Rate (2025-26):** 8.94% fixed (down from 9.08% in 2024-25)
- **Borrowing Limit:** Up to full cost of attendance minus other financial aid received
- **Origination Fee:** 4.228% — significantly higher than Unsubsidized loans
- **Credit Check:** Requires no adverse credit history (not a full credit score check)
- **CRITICAL UPDATE:** Grad PLUS loans are being eliminated effective July 1, 2026, under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. Students already enrolled and borrowing before that date are grandfathered for up to three additional academic years.

### Private Loans
- **Interest Rates:** Typically 3.23% to 17.99% APR depending on creditworthiness and cosigner status
- **Borrowing Limit:** Varies by lender, generally up to cost of attendance
- **Key Lenders for Medical Students:** SoFi, Sallie Mae, College Ave, Earnest
- **Cosigner Release:** Available from some lenders (e.g., Sallie Mae after 12 months, College Ave after half the repayment period)
- **Critical Difference from Federal:** No access to income-driven repayment, PSLF, deferment, or forbearance protections
- **Growing Importance:** With the elimination of Grad PLUS loans and new federal borrowing caps ($50,000/year, $200,000 lifetime for professional students starting July 2026), private loans will become necessary for many medical students whose costs exceed federal limits

### Institutional Loans
- **Source:** Offered directly by individual medical schools
- **Interest Rates:** Generally favorable, often below federal rates
- **Examples:** LDS loans, PCL loans, AMA-ERF loans, school-specific emergency or need-based loans
- **Availability:** Varies greatly by institution; not all schools offer them
- **Potential Restrictions:** Some institutional loans may include covenants requiring primary care practice in specific regions, with penalties (up to 15% interest) if conditions are not met
- **Note:** Perkins Loans (5% fixed, subsidized, 9-month grace period) are no longer being issued but some borrowers remain in repayment

## 3. Repayment Plans

### Non-Income-Driven Plans

**Standard Repayment Plan**
- Fixed monthly payments over 10 years (or up to 25 years based on loan balance under new rules)
- Lowest total interest paid
- Monthly payment example: $300K at 7% = ~$3,483/month
- Payments do NOT qualify efficiently for PSLF (nothing remains to forgive)

**Graduated Repayment Plan**
- Payments start low and increase every 2 years
- 10-year repayment term
- Higher total interest than Standard
- **Being eliminated** for new borrowers after July 1, 2026

**Extended Repayment Plan**
- Up to 25 years of repayment
- Requires $30,000+ in Direct or FFEL loans
- Lower monthly payments but significantly higher total cost
- **Being eliminated** for new borrowers after July 1, 2026

### Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) Plans

#### Income-Based Repayment (IBR) — CURRENTLY THE PRIMARY VIABLE IDR OPTION

| Feature | New IBR (post-July 2014 loans) | Old IBR (pre-July 2014 loans) |
|---------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Payment | 10% of discretionary income | 15% of discretionary income |
| Forgiveness | After 20 years | After 25 years |
| Cap | Standard 10-year payment amount | Standard 10-year payment amount |
| Requirement | Partial financial hardship | Partial financial hardship |

- IBR is currently the only IDR plan actively processing forgiveness
- The One Big Beautiful Bill now allows borrowers without partial financial hardship to enroll in IBR (as of December 22, 2025)
- **Best current choice for most residents pursuing PSLF**

#### Pay As You Earn (PAYE)
- 10% of discretionary income
- Capped at Standard 10-year payment
- Requires partial financial hardship
- 20-year forgiveness
- Still open to new enrollees through at least July 1, 2027
- **Sunsets by July 1, 2028** — borrowers will be transitioned to another plan
- Allows Married Filing Separately to exclude spouse's income

#### SAVE Plan (formerly REPAYE) — DEAD
- **Officially ended** by 8th Circuit Court of Appeals ruling on March 9, 2026
- More than 7 million borrowers were enrolled
- Borrowers in SAVE were placed in administrative forbearance during legal challenges
- Those borrowers must now transition to IBR or wait for RAP (July 2026)
- Interest accrued during the forbearance period

#### Income-Contingent Repayment (ICR)
- 20% of discretionary income or fixed 12-year payment adjusted for income, whichever is less
- 25-year forgiveness
- Primarily used by Parent PLUS borrowers who consolidated
- **Sunsets summer 2028**

### NEW: Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) — Available July 1, 2026

RAP will be the only IDR plan available to new borrowers after July 1, 2026.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Payment calculation | 1-10% of AGI (sliding scale based on earnings) |
| Dependent deduction | $50/month reduction per dependent |
| Minimum payment | $10/month |
| Interest treatment | Unpaid interest canceled monthly (balance cannot grow) |
| Principal guarantee | At least $50/month principal reduction (government subsidy if needed) |
| Forgiveness timeline | 30 years |
| PSLF eligible | Yes |
| Spouse income | Can file Married Filing Separately to exclude |
| Parent PLUS eligible | No |

**Resident payment example under RAP:** A resident earning $65,000 with one dependent would pay roughly 5-6% of AGI, approximately $270-$325/month, with any unpaid interest forgiven and guaranteed principal reduction.

### Transition Timeline
- **Now through June 2026:** IBR and PAYE are the main options; SAVE is dead
- **July 1, 2026:** RAP becomes available; Graduated and Extended plans eliminated for new borrowers
- **July 1, 2028:** PAYE and ICR sunset; all borrowers transitioned to IBR or RAP

## 4. Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)

### How It Works
After making **120 qualifying monthly payments** (10 years) while working full-time for a qualifying public service employer and repaying under a qualifying plan, the remaining Direct Loan balance is forgiven **tax-free**.

### Qualifying Employers for Residents
- **501(c)(3) nonprofit hospitals** — the vast majority of academic medical centers and teaching hospitals qualify
- Government hospitals (VA, military, public health service)
- Other nonprofit organizations
- **Key action:** Ask during residency interviews whether the program's employer qualifies; verify using the [Federal Student Aid employer search tool](https://studentaid.gov/manage-loans/forgiveness-cancellation/public-service)

### Qualifying Payments
- Must be made under a qualifying repayment plan (IDR plans or Standard — though Standard is not strategically recommended)
- Must be made while employed full-time by a qualifying employer
- Payments do not need to be consecutive
- Recent improvements allow lump-sum prepayments up to 12 months before income recertification
- Some deferments and forbearances may now count as qualifying payments

### The PSLF Math for Residents
A resident on IBR earning $65,000/year with $250,000 in debt would pay approximately $300-400/month during residency (3-7 years depending on specialty). These low payments count toward the 120 total. After completing residency and joining a qualifying employer as an attending, the remaining 3-7 years of payments would be at a higher amount, but the total forgiven could still be substantial — potentially $100,000-$300,000+ depending on specialty and training length.

### Certification Process
1. **Consolidate** any non-Direct loans into a Direct Consolidation Loan (FFEL or Perkins loans do not qualify directly)
2. **Enroll** in a qualifying IDR plan (IBR recommended currently)
3. **Submit the PSLF Employment Certification Form (ECF)** annually and each time you change employers
4. Use the **PSLF Help Tool** at studentaid.gov to generate pre-filled forms with digital signature capability
5. **Track payments** through studentaid.gov; keep personal records to verify accuracy
6. After 120 payments, submit the PSLF application for forgiveness

### CRITICAL CHANGE: Residency May No Longer Count (Post-July 2026 Borrowers)
Under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, for loans issued after July 1, 2026:
- **Time spent in residency or fellowship will no longer count toward the 120 qualifying PSLF payments**
- Doctors in training may need to spend additional years repaying debt before qualifying
- This effectively adds 3-7 years to the PSLF timeline for new borrowers
- Existing borrowers with loans disbursed before July 1, 2026 are grandfathered

## 5. Refinancing During Residency

### When to Refinance
- **During residency:** Only for private loans or if you are certain you will NOT pursue PSLF or IDR forgiveness
- **After residency (as attending):** Appropriate if entering private practice, debt-to-income ratio is below 1.0, and aggressive payoff is planned
- **Never refinance if:** You plan to work for a 501(c)(3) and pursue PSLF

### Major Refinancing Companies for Medical Professionals

| Company | Fixed Rate Range | Residency Payment | Key Feature |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **SoFi** | From 3.62% APR | $100/month for up to 7 years | No fees; 5-20 year terms; interest capitalizes at end of residency |
| **Laurel Road** (KeyBank) | Competitive | $100/month | Interest does NOT capitalize during residency; no origination fees |
| **Earnest** | 4.60%-10.24% APR (4.35%-9.99% with autopay) | Varies | Best overall ratings; flexible terms; accepts 665+ credit |
| **Splash Financial** | Varies | Varies | Medical school specialization; marketplace model |
| **ELFI** | Varies | Varies | High balance specialists |

### Pros of Refinancing During Residency
- Potentially lower interest rates (3-6%) compared to federal rates (7.94-8.94%)
- Resident-specific programs with $100/month payments during training
- No origination fees from most lenders
- Can lock in favorable fixed rates
- Reduced total interest cost over loan lifetime

### Cons of Refinancing During Residency
- **Permanently loses federal protections:** IDR plans, PSLF eligibility, deferment, forbearance
- $100/month likely does not cover accrued interest, causing negative amortization
- With SoFi, accrued interest capitalizes when regular payments begin (Laurel Road is an exception)
- Career plans may change — refinancing locks you out of PSLF even if you later join a nonprofit
- If you refinance and then face financial hardship, private loans have no safety net

### Decision Framework
- **Debt-to-income ratio > 2:1** (e.g., $400K debt, $200K salary): PSLF likely saves more
- **Debt-to-income ratio < 1:1** (e.g., $200K debt, $300K salary): Refinance and pay aggressively
- **Training > 5 years at qualifying employer:** PSLF almost always wins
- **Going into private practice:** Refinance after residency for best rates

## 6. Grace Periods and Forbearance

### Grace Period
- **Direct Unsubsidized Loans:** 6-month grace period after graduation or dropping below half-time enrollment
- **Perkins Loans:** 9-month grace period (for legacy borrowers)
- During grace period, interest accrues on Unsubsidized and Grad PLUS loans
- The grace period provides time to transition from medical school to residency

### Forbearance During Residency

**Mandatory Residency Forbearance:**
- Medical and dental residents are entitled to mandatory forbearance if they request it
- Approved in annual increments — must be resubmitted each year
- During forbearance, no payments are required

**The True Cost of Forbearance:**
- Interest continues to accrue on all federal loan types during forbearance
- Example: $200,000 in debt at 7% accrues approximately $14,000/year in interest
- Over a 3-year residency on forbearance: approximately $42,000+ in accrued interest
- This interest capitalizes (adds to principal) when repayment resumes
- Concrete example from Student Loan Planner: $192,000 at 6.5% over 3 years of forbearance = $233,217 total balance ($41,217 in added interest)

**Why Forbearance Is Almost Always a Mistake:**
- Prevents qualifying payments toward PSLF
- Adds tens of thousands in capitalized interest
- IDR payments during residency are typically very low ($0-$400/month based on resident salary) and count toward forgiveness
- Every month in forbearance is a wasted month that could have counted toward the 120 PSLF payments

**Recommended Alternative:**
- Enroll in IBR (or PAYE/RAP when available) immediately upon entering residency
- File taxes for the prior year (final year of med school) to demonstrate low income and secure the lowest possible IDR payment
- If married, consider Married Filing Separately to exclude spouse's income from IDR calculation

## 7. Current Legislative Landscape (as of March 2026)

### The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Signed July 4, 2025)
This sweeping legislation represents the most significant overhaul of federal student loan programs in decades.

**Key Changes Effective July 1, 2026:**

| Change | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Grad PLUS elimination | No new Grad PLUS loans; existing borrowers grandfathered for up to 3 years |
| New borrowing caps | $50,000/year, $200,000 lifetime for professional students |
| Graduated/Extended plans eliminated | No longer available to new borrowers |
| RAP replaces most IDR plans | Sole IDR option for new borrowers (1-10% of AGI, 30-year forgiveness) |
| PSLF residency exclusion | Residency/fellowship no longer counts toward 120 qualifying payments for new borrowers |
| Parent PLUS caps | $20,000/year, $65,000 lifetime per dependent |

**Changes Effective July 1, 2028:**
- PAYE and ICR plans sunset
- All borrowers transitioned to IBR or RAP

### SAVE Plan Litigation — Resolved
- The 8th Circuit Court of Appeals definitively ended the SAVE plan on March 9, 2026
- 7+ million enrolled borrowers must transition to IBR or RAP
- The Department of Education agreed to halt all SAVE enrollment and forgiveness
- Borrowers in SAVE during the legal battle were in administrative forbearance — interest accrued but no IDR credit was earned

### Resident Interest Freeze Proposal
- The AMA has advocated for legislation to freeze student loan interest during residency
- A bill was proposed but has not been enacted as of March 2026

### Impact on Future Medical Students
Students entering medical school in fall 2026 or later face a fundamentally different financing landscape:
- Maximum federal borrowing of $200,000 vs. median 4-year cost of $286,454 (public) or $390,848 (private)
- The gap must be filled with private loans, institutional loans, or personal savings
- Private loans lack IDR, PSLF, deferment, and forbearance protections
- PSLF becomes less valuable with mixed federal/private portfolios and residency exclusion
- The calculus shifts toward aggressive repayment and away from forgiveness-dependent strategies

## 8. Common Mistakes Residents Make with Their Loans

### Mistake 1: Choosing Forbearance Over IDR
The single most expensive mistake. Forbearance during a 3-5 year residency can add $40,000-$70,000+ in capitalized interest AND wastes 36-60 months that could count toward PSLF. Always enroll in IBR or another qualifying IDR plan instead.

### Mistake 2: Refinancing When PSLF Would Save More
Refinancing federal loans into private loans permanently eliminates PSLF eligibility. For a physician with $300,000 in debt who will work at a nonprofit hospital, PSLF could forgive $100,000-$300,000+. Refinancing that away for a 2% rate reduction is catastrophically expensive. **Rule of thumb:** If your debt-to-income ratio exceeds 1.5:1 and you will work for a qualifying employer, pursue PSLF.

### Mistake 3: Not Filing Taxes Strategically
- Failing to file a tax return for the final year of medical school means IDR payments will be calculated using residency pay stubs instead of the prior year's (much lower) income
- Filing Married Filing Jointly when Married Filing Separately would yield lower IDR payments
- Not understanding that PAYE and RAP allow spousal income exclusion when filing separately

### Mistake 4: Not Submitting PSLF Employment Certification Annually
Waiting until the end of 10 years to submit certification risks discovering that payments did not qualify. Submit the ECF every year and every time you change employers to track progress and catch issues early.

### Mistake 5: Consolidating Federal Loans After Starting PSLF Payments
Consolidation resets the PSLF payment counter to zero. Never consolidate loans that already have qualifying PSLF payments unless you have no other choice (e.g., converting FFEL loans to Direct).

### Mistake 6: Lifestyle Inflation ("Growing Into Income")
Transitioning from a $60,000 resident salary to a $250,000-$500,000 attending salary creates enormous temptation to spend. The White Coat Investor's core principle: **"Live like a resident"** for 2-5 years after completing training. This discipline can enable paying off $200,000-$400,000 in debt in 2-5 years.

### Mistake 7: Not Understanding the Federal vs. Private Distinction
Treating all loans the same. Federal and private loans have fundamentally different features. Keep federal loans federal (for protections and forgiveness) and refinance only private loans during residency. Only refinance federal loans after careful analysis confirms PSLF/IDR is not beneficial.

### Mistake 8: Ignoring Loan Servicer Communications
Missing recertification deadlines for IDR plans can cause payment amounts to spike to the Standard plan amount. Missing PSLF forms can result in lost qualifying payment credit. Stay engaged with your servicer.

## Summary Decision Matrix for New Residents

| Scenario | Recommended Strategy |
|----------|---------------------|
| Residency at 501(c)(3) + plan to stay at nonprofit | Enroll in IBR, pursue PSLF, submit ECF annually |
| Residency at 501(c)(3) + plan to go private practice | Enroll in IBR during residency (keeps options open), then refinance as attending |
| Residency at for-profit employer | Enroll in IBR for low payments; evaluate refinancing post-residency |
| Debt-to-income < 1:1, going private practice | Consider refinancing after residency for aggressive payoff |
| Have private loans from med school | Refinance private loans now for lower rate; keep federal loans on IDR |
| New borrower starting July 2026+ | Only option will be Standard or RAP; PSLF residency years won't count |
