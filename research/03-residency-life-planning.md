## 1. The Match and Transition Timeline

### Key Dates (Using 2026 Cycle as Reference)

| Date | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| June 5, 2025 | ERAS season opens for applicant registration |
| September 3, 2025 | Applications can be submitted via MyERAS |
| September 24, 2025 | Programs begin reviewing applications |
| November 2025 - January 2026 | Interview season (virtual and in-person) |
| February 2, 2026 | Rank order list submission opens |
| March 4, 2026 | Deadline to certify rank order list |
| March 16, 2026 (Monday of Match Week) | Applicants learn whether they matched (but not where) |
| March 16-20, 2026 | SOAP (Supplemental Offer and Acceptance Program) for unmatched applicants |
| March 20, 2026 (Friday - Match Day) | Match results revealed at 12:00 PM ET |
| Late April - May 2026 | Medical school graduation |
| Late June 2026 | Orientation at residency program |
| July 1, 2026 | Residency officially begins |

### The Transition Window: Match Day to July 1 (~3.5 Months)

This is one of the most compressed and high-stakes transition periods in any career. In roughly 100 days, you must:

1. **Celebrate and process** - Match Day itself is emotional regardless of outcome
2. **Complete medical school** - Final rotations, exams, graduation requirements
3. **Find housing** in your new city (often a city you have never lived in)
4. **Obtain state medical licensure** - Training permit or full license in your new state
5. **Complete credentialing** - Hospital privileges, background checks, drug screens
6. **Handle DEA registration** if required by your program
7. **Move** - Physically relocate your entire life
8. **Orientation** - Usually the last week of June, covering EMR training, compliance, etc.
9. **Start working** - July 1, Day One as a physician

### Critical Post-Match Checklist

- [ ] Contact your program coordinator within 48 hours of matching
- [ ] Begin housing search immediately (within the first week)
- [ ] Start state medical license application (can take 4-12 weeks)
- [ ] Complete all credentialing paperwork sent by the program
- [ ] Set up bank accounts in new city if needed
- [ ] Arrange moving logistics (truck, movers, shipping)
- [ ] Forward mail and update address
- [ ] Transfer prescriptions to new pharmacy
- [ ] Secure health insurance (gap coverage if needed before program benefits start)
- [ ] Obtain new state driver's license and vehicle registration (varies by state)

## 2. Housing During Residency

### When and How to Find Housing

**Timeline:**
- Begin searching immediately after Match Day (mid-March)
- Aim to secure housing by mid-May at the latest
- Many residents sign leases sight-unseen or via virtual tours
- If possible, visit the city during interview season and scout neighborhoods

**Strategies from Real Residents (SDN Forums):**
- Reach out to current residents at your program -- they are the best source of neighborhood recommendations, commute times, and landlord reviews
- Many programs maintain informal housing lists or Facebook groups for incoming residents
- Use apartments.com, Zillow, Craigslist, and local Facebook groups
- Ask the program coordinator if any outgoing residents have leases to take over
- "Any place worth renting should have a listing agent who can do a FaceTime tour" (SDN advice)
- If you are only in a location for one year (preliminary/transitional), consider furnished apartments

**Proximity to Hospital:**
- Living close to the hospital is generally recommended, especially for surgical specialties and any program with frequent in-house call
- Shorter commute = more sleep = better performance and wellness
- However, housing near major medical centers often carries a premium
- Some residents prefer living 15-20 minutes away to separate work and home life

### Rent vs. Buy Analysis

**The consensus from financial advisors (White Coat Investor, Student Loan Planner, financial forums) is overwhelmingly: RENT during residency.** Here is why:

**Arguments Against Buying:**
1. **Short timeline** - You need 3-5 years minimum to break even on a home purchase after transaction costs (closing costs, agent fees eat ~15% of home value)
2. **Insufficient down payment** - Without 20% down, you face PMI and greater risk
3. **Existing debt** - Average medical school debt exceeds $200,000-$250,000
4. **Hidden costs** - Maintenance, property taxes, insurance, and furnishing costs are consistently underestimated
5. **Lifestyle upgrade inevitable** - Most physicians move or upgrade after residency
6. **Time drain** - Home maintenance takes limited free time away from rest and study
7. **Minimal tax benefit** - Standard deduction typically exceeds itemized mortgage interest deductions at resident income levels
8. **Location uncertainty** - Fellowship or attending job may be in a different city
9. **You need the home to appreciate ~4% annually during residency just to break even in 3 years**

**When Buying Might Make Sense:**
- You are in a 5-7 year residency AND plan to stay in the area afterward
- You have a partner with stable income and a down payment
- You are in a low cost-of-living area with favorable rent-to-buy ratios
- Physician mortgage loans (0-10% down, no PMI) make it feasible, but feasible does not mean wise

### Budget Guidelines

- Ideal: spend no more than 25-35% of gross monthly income on housing
- Reality: nearly 60% of residents are "rent-burdened" (spending 30%+ of take-home pay on rent)
- Getting roommates is very common, especially in high cost-of-living cities
- In cities like San Francisco and New York, some residents live with 4-8 roommates
- Moving allowances are available at only ~29% of programs; housing stipends at ~14%

### Resident Salary Reference (2025-2026)

| PGY Level | National Average | Example (RUSH) |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| PGY-1 | ~$66,000-$68,000 | $71,657 |
| PGY-2 | ~$68,000-$72,000 | $76,374 |
| PGY-3 | ~$70,000-$75,000 | $78,799 |
| PGY-4 | ~$73,000-$78,000 | $82,839 |

Pay increases approximately $2,000-$5,000 per year. Benefits (health insurance, meal allowances, education funds, retirement contributions) significantly affect total compensation.

## 3. Step 3 / COMLEX Level 3

### USMLE Step 3

**When to Take It:**
- Most programs require passing Step 3 during intern year to advance to PGY-2
- Recommended timing: during an elective block, outpatient rotation, or consult service (you will not need to find coverage)
- Do not put it off too long -- clinical knowledge from medical school gets rusty, especially in subspecialties far from internal medicine
- Earlier in intern year is generally better than later

**Preparation:**
- Recommended preparation window: 90-120 days of passive study (doing questions daily alongside clinical work)
- Dedicated study: at least 3-4 weeks of focused preparation
- If you struggle with standardized tests or are in a highly specialized field, begin 6 months out
- Whatever study method worked for Step 1/Step 2, adapt it to your residency schedule

**Key Study Resources:**
- **UWorld Step 3 QBank** - The gold standard, used by the vast majority of residents
- **NBME Self-Assessments** - Official practice exams for score calibration
- **CCS (Computer-based Case Simulations)** - Practice with official USMLE sample cases; this component is unique to Step 3
- **USMLE Step 3 Content Outline** - Available on the USMLE website; familiarize yourself with tested topics

**Exam Format:**
- Two-day exam (Day 1: MCQs focused on foundational medicine; Day 2: MCQs + CCS cases)
- CCS cases require practice -- they test clinical decision-making in a simulated environment
- Pass rate is high (>95%) but preparation is still essential

**Practical Tips:**
- Schedule your exam date early in the year when you see your rotation schedule
- Many residents study during downtime on lighter rotations
- Podcasts and audio resources work well for commute-time studying
- Consider scheduling both exam days around a day off

### COMLEX Level 3 (for DO Students)

**When to Take It:**
- NBOME recommends waiting at least 6 months after starting residency
- Most DOs take it during PGY-1 year
- Each program may have its own internal deadlines -- check with your program

**Preparation:**
- Start reviewing and take a diagnostic test before your program starts
- Draft a study plan before beginning residency and adjust as you go

**Key Study Resources:**
- COMBANK (TrueLearn) or COMQUEST question banks
- OMT Review: A Comprehensive Review in Osteopathic Medicine
- All USMLE Step 3 resources are also relevant for the clinical components

**Important Note:** Many DO students also take USMLE Step 3 in addition to COMLEX Level 3, especially if they want maximum flexibility for licensure across states. Check your state's requirements.

## 4. Residency Structure by Specialty

### Residency Length by Specialty

| Specialty | Total Training Length |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Family Medicine | 3 years |
| Internal Medicine | 3 years |
| Pediatrics | 3 years |
| Emergency Medicine | 3-4 years |
| Psychiatry | 4 years |
| OB/GYN | 4 years |
| Anesthesiology | 4 years (3 + prelim year) |
| Pathology | 4 years |
| Dermatology | 4 years (3 + prelim year) |
| Neurology | 4 years (3 + prelim year) |
| Physical Medicine & Rehab | 3-4 years |
| General Surgery | 5 years |
| Orthopedic Surgery | 5 years |
| Otolaryngology (ENT) | 5 years |
| Urology | 5 years |
| Ophthalmology | 4 years (3 + prelim year) |
| Diagnostic Radiology | 5 years (4 + prelim year) |
| Radiation Oncology | 5 years (4 + prelim year) |
| Plastic Surgery | 6 years |
| Neurosurgery | 7 years |

### Position Types Explained

- **Categorical**: Full-length position that provides complete training for board certification
- **Preliminary**: One-year position (in Internal Medicine, Surgery, or Transitional Year) that serves as a prerequisite for advanced positions
- **Transitional Year**: A one-year broad-based clinical experience, often preceding advanced specialty training
- **Advanced**: Positions that begin at PGY-2 or later, requiring completion of a preliminary year elsewhere

### Internal Medicine (3 Years) - Year by Year

**PGY-1 (Intern Year):**
- Heaviest clinical workload of the three years
- Many programs use a "3+1" model: three-week inpatient blocks followed by one-week ambulatory blocks
- Rotations: General medicine wards, MICU, CCU, cardiology, neurology, hematology/oncology
- Continuity clinic: Half-day of primary care clinic weekly throughout the year
- You are the primary physician for your patients -- writing orders, developing care plans, presenting on rounds
- Supervised by PGY-2/3 residents and attending physicians

**PGY-2 (Second Year):**
- Dual role: direct patient care AND supervision of interns and medical students
- Approximately 3 months of ward service (as senior resident)
- Increased subspecialty elective time
- Begin thinking about fellowship vs. hospitalist vs. outpatient career
- If pursuing fellowship: begin identifying mentors, starting research

**PGY-3 (Third Year):**
- Most senior resident on the team; runs inpatient services
- Supervises PGY-1s and medical students in both wards and ICU
- Significant elective and subspecialty time
- Fellowship applications submitted (if pursuing) during PGY-2/early PGY-3
- Teaching responsibilities increase substantially
- Board preparation begins in earnest

### General Surgery (5 Years) - Year by Year

**PGY-1 (Intern Year):**
- Rotations across general surgery, vascular, acute care/trauma, oncology, transplant
- Primary responsibilities: pre- and post-operative patient care, ward management
- Assisting in the OR; performing simple procedures under supervision
- Long hours; often the most demanding intern year of any specialty

**PGY-2:**
- Rotations: breast surgery, SICU, surgical oncology, transplant, pediatric surgery
- Begin performing more complex procedures with increasing independence
- Float/cross-cover responsibilities

**PGY-3 (Mid-Level):**
- Serve as the mid-level resident on surgical teams
- Rotations: cardiothoracic, pediatric/general surgery, surgical oncology
- Research year at some programs (dedicated or integrated research time)
- Growing operative independence

**PGY-4:**
- Float chief responsibilities
- SICU, surgical oncology, vascular, colorectal surgery rotations
- Significant operative caseload
- Mentoring junior residents

**PGY-5 (Chief Year):**
- Direct a surgical service under attending supervision
- Rotations: oncology, trauma, hepatobiliary, general surgery
- Maximal operative independence
- Train and evaluate junior residents and medical students
- Board preparation for the American Board of Surgery qualifying exam

### Emergency Medicine (3-4 Years)

- 13-block schedule per year balancing ED months with off-service rotations
- During ED months: typically 18-21 shifts of 8-12 hours each
- Over full residency: ~27 months in the ED, 5+ months in various ICUs
- Off-service rotations: trauma, pediatric EM, critical care, EMS, ultrasound, toxicology
- Shift-based schedule (no traditional "call") -- but irregular hours and night shifts are the norm
- Career development time built into senior years
- Shift count decreases slightly as seniority increases

### Pediatrics (3 Years)

**PGY-1:** Inpatient floors, primary care, NICU, newborn nursery, Emergency Department -- focused on acute and chronic illness management

**PGY-2:** Additional clinical skills, developing independence, leadership and supervision roles, subspecialty exposure

**PGY-3:** Supervisory experiences on inpatient and specialty teams, ambulatory care, teaching skill development, evidence-based medicine proficiency

## 5. Work Hours and Lifestyle

### ACGME Duty Hour Requirements (Current Standards)

These are the federally enforced rules that all ACGME-accredited residency programs must follow:

| Rule | Requirement |
|------|------------|
| **Maximum weekly hours** | 80 hours/week, averaged over 4 weeks. Includes all clinical work, education, and moonlighting |
| **Continuous duty** | No more than 24 hours of continuous scheduled clinical work |
| **Extended duty** | May stay up to 4 additional hours (28 hours total) for didactics, care transitions, and continuity of care |
| **Minimum time off between shifts** | 8 hours off between scheduled clinical work periods |
| **Days off** | Minimum 1 day in 7 free from all clinical/educational duties, averaged over 4 weeks. One day = continuous 24-hour period |
| **In-house call frequency** | No more frequently than every third night, averaged over 4 weeks |
| **Moonlighting** | Counts toward the 80-hour weekly limit |
| **At-home call** | Not subject to every-third-night limitation, but must still allow 1 day in 7 free |
| **Exceptions** | Some specialties may request up to 88 hours/week with educational justification |

### What "80 Hours" Actually Looks Like

**Important reality check:** The 80-hour limit is a *maximum*, not a target. However, many programs schedule close to this limit, and actual hours often exceed it.

**Typical Weekly Schedules by Specialty:**
- **Internal Medicine wards:** 60-80 hours/week. Arrive 5-6 AM, round, manage patients, afternoon didactics, leave 5-7 PM. Weekends on call.
- **General Surgery:** 70-90+ hours/week (violations are common, often underreported). In early at 4-5 AM for pre-rounding, OR during the day, post-op checks and notes in evening.
- **Emergency Medicine:** 40-60 hours/week of shift work, but shifts include nights, weekends, and holidays with irregular schedules.
- **Family Medicine:** 50-70 hours/week. Generally more predictable with clinic-based schedules.
- **Psychiatry:** 50-65 hours/week. Among the more lifestyle-friendly residencies.

### Night Float

- Many programs use a "night float" system instead of traditional 24-hour call
- Typically 1-2 week blocks of overnight shifts (6 PM - 6 AM or similar)
- Residents cover admissions and emergencies overnight
- Allows day team to go home at reasonable hours
- Night float months are physically demanding but usually have fewer total days worked

### Call Schedules

- **In-house call:** Physically present in the hospital overnight (typically q4 or q5 nights)
- **Home call:** On-call from home; respond to phone calls and come in if needed
- **24+4 call:** Work a full day, stay overnight, can remain up to 4 additional hours the next day (up to 28 hours total)
- **Short call/Late call:** Extended shifts without overnight stay (e.g., staying until 9-10 PM)

### Duty Hour Violations

- Programs are required to track and report duty hours
- Residents can (and should) report violations to the ACGME
- Violations can result in citations and adverse accreditation actions
- Reality: underreporting of hours is a known systemic issue; culture can discourage reporting
- The ACGME has an anonymous reporting mechanism

## 6. Wellness and Burnout

### The Scale of the Problem

**Overall prevalence:** Nearly 50% of both practicing physicians and residents experience burnout. This exceeds rates seen in similarly aged non-physician populations.

**Burnout rates increase dramatically during training:**
- At the start of internship: ~4.3% meet burnout criteria
- By end of intern year: ~55.3% meet burnout criteria (Internal Medicine data)

**Burnout by Specialty (Martini et al. study):**
| Specialty | Burnout Rate |
|-----------|-------------|
| OB/GYN | 75% |
| Internal Medicine | 63% |
| Neurology | 63% |
| Ophthalmology | 60% |
| Dermatology | 50% |
| General Surgery | 40% |
| Psychiatry | 40% |
| Family Medicine | 27% |

### Three Dimensions of Burnout (Maslach Model)

1. **Emotional exhaustion** - Feeling drained, overwhelmed, unable to give more
2. **Depersonalization/Cynicism** - Detachment from patients, viewing them as cases rather than people
3. **Reduced personal accomplishment** - Feeling ineffective, questioning competence and career choice

### Risk Factors

**Work-related:**
- Sleep deprivation (the single largest contributor)
- Excessive workload and long hours
- Lack of schedule control and autonomy
- Conflicts with co-workers or supervisors
- Administrative burden and EMR documentation
- Demanding patient responsibilities

**Personal factors:**
- Younger age
- Female gender (higher rates of emotional exhaustion)
- Being unmarried or without strong social support
- Neurotic or introverted personality traits
- Pre-existing mental health conditions
- Financial stress from student loans

### Consequences

- **Medical errors:** 11% increased likelihood per point increase in depersonalization; 5% per point increase in emotional exhaustion
- **Mental health:** Depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation
- **Physical health:** Cardiovascular disease, substance abuse, weight gain
- **Career impact:** Absenteeism, reduced patient interaction, early career exit
- **Patient safety:** Reduced compassion, decreased quality of care

### Mental Health Resources

**Available through most programs:**
- Employee Assistance Programs (EAP) - free, confidential counseling (typically 3-8 sessions)
- Program-specific wellness committees and wellness days
- Peer support groups for residents
- Mentorship programs with senior residents and faculty
- Chaplain and spiritual care services

**External resources:**
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** (call or text 988)
- **Physician Support Line:** 1-888-409-0141 (free, confidential peer support by psychiatrists)
- **Dr. Lorna Breen Heroes' Foundation** - advocacy for physician mental health
- **State physician health programs** - confidential support in every state

**Barriers to accessing help (and how to overcome them):**
- *Time:* Look for programs offering teletherapy/virtual counseling
- *Stigma:* Remember that most state licensing boards do NOT ask about mental health treatment -- only about impairment
- *Confidentiality concerns:* EAP and external therapists are confidential; many are outside the hospital system
- *Cost:* EAP is free; many programs now offer therapy stipends

### Evidence-Based Coping Strategies

1. **Exercise** - Even 20 minutes/day or every other day makes a significant difference
2. **Social connection** - Spending time with family/friends once a week; 58% of residents cite this as their top coping strategy
3. **Mindfulness and meditation** - 8-week mindfulness programs (2.5 hours/week) show measurable reduction in burnout
4. **Hobbies** - Maintaining at least one non-medical interest (cooking, music, gardening, reading)
5. **Sleep hygiene** - Prioritize sleep over almost everything else
6. **Therapy** - Proactive, not just reactive. Consider starting before burnout develops
7. **Peer support groups** - Normalize the experience; Balint groups specifically designed for this
8. **Setting boundaries** - Learn to say no to non-essential commitments
9. **Meal support** - Rated as one of the most helpful institutional interventions
10. **Mentorship** - Regular check-ins with a trusted mentor who has been through it

## 7. Relationships During Residency

### The Reality

- About one-third of residents will be geographically separated from their partner at some point during training
- Residency is hard on relationships, but it is not a death sentence -- many physicians maintain and strengthen relationships during training
- The key variables: communication, flexibility, realistic expectations, and mutual commitment

### Couples Match

**How it works:**
- Two applicants link their rank order lists through the NRMP R3 system
- The algorithm evaluates linked pairs simultaneously, working through every combination to find the best mutual outcome
- Rather than each person matching independently, results are determined together
- NRMP limits rank lists to 300 paired ranks

**Success rates:**
- The couples match rate has never dropped below 90% since 1987
- In 2025: 93.2% overall; 89.1% of couples had both partners match
- 8.1% had one partner match and one not; 2.8% had neither match

**Strategic advice:**
- Apply more broadly than you would individually -- more programs = more pair combinations
- Discuss your relationship commitment, specialty preferences, and geographic flexibility BEFORE starting
- Communicate your couples match status during interviews
- Couples match applicants tend to match lower on their lists than individual applicants
- Calculate distance between programs in each pair to assess feasibility
- Consider adding "no match" as one partner's option in some pairs (e.g., Partner A matches, Partner B takes a gap year and reapplies)

### Managing Relationships During Residency

**For couples living together:**
- Share your call schedule/rotation schedule with your partner (add them to schedule emails)
- Protect your days off -- even partial days together matter
- Set realistic expectations about availability (you will miss dinners, birthdays, holidays)
- Find small daily rituals: morning coffee, brief phone call after work, etc.
- Divide household responsibilities realistically -- the non-resident partner often carries more at home
- Schedule date nights and protect them like you would a meeting

**For long-distance relationships:**
- Call your partner as soon as you leave the hospital to share day-to-day experiences
- Plan visits multiple months in advance -- always have the next visit on the calendar
- Take vacations together; coordinate your limited vacation time
- Discuss what is equitable regarding travel burden so it does not always fall on one person
- Consider couples counseling before the separation to solidify your foundation
- Have an end date or plan -- knowing when the distance will end helps enormously
- Video calls > text messages for maintaining emotional connection

**Dating during residency:**
- It is possible but requires intentionality
- Dating apps can be efficient given limited free time
- Be upfront about your schedule and availability
- Non-medical partners may need extra context about why your schedule is unpredictable
- Some residents prefer dating other medical professionals who "get it"

### When to Seek Help

- Relationship counseling is not a sign of failure -- it is a tool
- Many EAPs offer couples counseling sessions
- Address issues early rather than letting resentment build during an already stressful time

## 8. Moving Logistics and Licensure

### Medical Licensure for New Residents

**Training Permit vs. Full Medical License:**
- Most states issue a training permit or limited license for residents, allowing supervised practice
- A full, unrestricted medical license typically requires: completion of at least 1-3 years of GME (varies by state) + passing all USMLE/COMLEX steps
- Some states allow a full license after just PGY-1; others require PGY-2 or PGY-3 completion
- Having a full license enables moonlighting

**Application process:**
- Start your state medical license application immediately after Match Day
- Processing times vary: 4-12 weeks depending on the state
- Required documents: medical school diploma, transcripts, USMLE/COMLEX scores, dean's letter, background check
- Many states require fingerprinting
- Application fees: typically $200-$600

**Interstate Medical Licensure Compact (IMLC):**
- 42 states + DC + Guam are members (as of 2026)
- Expedites obtaining licenses in multiple states
- Requires establishing a State of Principal Licensure (SPL) based on: primary residence, practice location, employer location, or tax residence
- Useful for telehealth, moonlighting in adjacent states, or future career moves
- When you move states, you can redesignate your SPL

### DEA Registration

- **During residency:** Individual DEA registration is NOT required if you practice under your hospital/program's institutional DEA registration
- **You WILL need your own DEA if:** you moonlight outside your program, or when you complete training and enter independent practice
- **Application:** DEA Form 224 (online at deadiversion.usdoj.gov); requires active state medical license
- **Training requirement:** As of June 2024, new/renewing DEA registrants need 8 hours of training on opioid/substance use disorders. Residency training typically satisfies this requirement.
- **State controlled substance permits:** About half of states require a separate state-level controlled substance registration in addition to the federal DEA
- **PDMP registration:** Many states require registration with the Prescription Drug Monitoring Program within 30 days of obtaining a DEA license

### State-Specific Requirements Checklist

- [ ] State medical license or training permit
- [ ] State controlled substance registration (if applicable)
- [ ] DEA registration (if moonlighting or required by program)
- [ ] PDMP registration
- [ ] NPI number (usually obtained in medical school; update address)
- [ ] Malpractice insurance (usually provided by program)
- [ ] Hospital credentialing and privileging
- [ ] State driver's license (most states require within 30-90 days of establishing residency)
- [ ] Vehicle registration and inspection
- [ ] Voter registration update

### Physical Moving Tips

- Get quotes from at least 3 moving companies; many offer physician discounts
- Consider PODS/portable containers for flexible timing
- Check if your program offers a moving allowance (~29% of programs do, typically $1,000-$3,000)
- Ship heavy items; drive with essentials
- If moving cross-country, consider whether driving or flying + shipping is more cost-effective
- Keep important documents (license applications, credentialing paperwork, diplomas) with you, not in the moving truck
- Set up utilities, internet, and mail forwarding before arrival
- Arrive at least 3-5 days before orientation starts

## 9. What the Next 3-7 Years Look Like

### The Big Picture Timeline

```
Year 1 (PGY-1): Intern year - steepest learning curve, hardest year for most
Year 2 (PGY-2): Increased autonomy, begin supervising, fellowship decisions
Year 3 (PGY-3): Senior resident, leadership roles, fellowship applications (if applicable)
Year 4-7 (PGY-4+): Chief year (surgery), fellowship, or completion of longer residencies
Post-training: Board certification exam, first attending job
```

### For a 3-Year Residency (e.g., Internal Medicine, Family Medicine, Pediatrics)

**Year 1 - Intern Year:**
- Focus: survival, learning systems, building clinical foundations
- Milestones: Pass Step 3/COMLEX Level 3, complete ACGME milestones
- Key decision: Am I pursuing fellowship or going straight to practice?

**Year 2:**
- Focus: developing clinical judgment, supervising interns
- If fellowship-bound: identify mentors, begin research, draft personal statement
- Fellowship applications open in spring/summer of PGY-2
- Begin studying for board certification

**Year 3:**
- Focus: leadership, teaching, career preparation
- Fellowship interviews (fall of PGY-3 if applied during PGY-2)
- Start job search if not pursuing fellowship (job search typically begins 12-18 months before completion)
- Board exam preparation intensifies
- Contract review and negotiation for first attending position

### For a 5-Year Residency (e.g., General Surgery, Orthopedics)

**Years 1-2:** Foundation building, basic procedures, ward management
**Year 3:** Mid-level responsibilities, often includes dedicated research time
**Years 4-5:** Senior/chief responsibilities, maximal operative independence
**Fellowship decisions** made during PGY-3 or PGY-4

### Fellowship Application Timeline

If you are pursuing fellowship from a 3-year residency:

| When | What |
|------|------|
| PGY-1 / Early PGY-2 | Identify subspecialty interest, find research mentors |
| Spring PGY-2 | Begin fellowship application preparation, secure letter writers |
| Early July PGY-2 | Letters of recommendation due to ERAS |
| July-August PGY-2 | ERAS fellowship application opens; submit within first 48-72 hours |
| Late August - November | Fellowship interview season |
| November-December | Rank list submission and Match |
| PGY-3 completion | Begin fellowship (PGY-4) |

### Fellowship Duration by Subspecialty (Selected)

| Subspecialty | Fellowship Length |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Cardiology | 3 years |
| Gastroenterology | 3 years |
| Pulmonary/Critical Care | 3 years |
| Hematology/Oncology | 3 years |
| Endocrinology | 2 years |
| Rheumatology | 2 years |
| Infectious Disease | 2 years |
| Nephrology | 2 years |
| Sports Medicine | 1 year |
| Hospice/Palliative | 1 year |

### The Attending Job Search

- Begin 12-18 months before training completion
- Engage with recruiters, attend specialty conferences, network
- Consider: academic vs. private practice, urban vs. rural, employed vs. partnership
- Contract review by a physician-specialized attorney is strongly recommended
- Negotiate: salary, sign-on bonus, loan repayment, call schedule, partnership track, tail coverage

## 10. Board Certification

### Timeline

- **During residency:** Prepare for specialty board exams throughout training
- **Final year of residency:** Apply for board certification examination
- **After residency:** Take the certifying exam (timing varies by specialty)
- **Board eligibility window:** 3-7 years after completing training to pass the exam (varies by ABMS member board)

### Requirements

1. Complete an ACGME-accredited residency program
2. Obtain attestation letters from program director and/or faculty
3. Hold an unrestricted medical license in the US or Canada
4. Pass the specialty certification examination

### Costs

| Component | Cost |
|-----------|------|
| Initial certification exam | Mean: $2,942 (varies by specialty) |
| Annual continuous certification (MOC) | Mean: $263/year |
| 10-year total MOC compliance | $23,607 - $40,495 depending on specialty |

### Exam Characteristics

- Mean exam: ~300 questions over 6 hours
- Initial certification pass rate: ~89%
- Continuous certification: ~49 questions, 4 minutes per question, 93% pass rate

### Specialty-Specific Notes

**Internal Medicine (ABIM):**
- Can take the certifying exam in the final months of PGY-3 or shortly after
- Computer-based exam, one day
- Most residents use MKSAP (Medical Knowledge Self-Assessment Program) for preparation

**General Surgery (ABS):**
- Two-part process: Qualifying Exam (written, taken in final year) + Certifying Exam (oral, taken after passing qualifying exam, typically 1-2 years post-residency)

**Family Medicine (ABFM):**
- Can sit for the exam during the last year of residency or shortly after

### Maintenance of Certification (MOC)

- Required by all ABMS boards for ongoing certification
- Involves: continuing medical education, periodic assessments, practice improvement activities
- Controversy exists around the value and burden of MOC
- Some states have attempted to limit MOC requirements through legislation

## 11. Networking and Career Planning

### Building Your CV During Residency

**Categories to actively build:**

1. **Research & Publications**
   - Even one case report or quality improvement project adds significant value
   - Collaborate with faculty on existing projects (lower activation energy than starting your own)
   - Aim for at least one publication and one presentation before fellowship applications
   - Track all contributions: posters, oral presentations, manuscripts, book chapters

2. **Conference Presentations**
   - Present at local, regional, and national conferences
   - Poster presentations are more accessible early in training
   - Oral presentations carry more weight but are more competitive
   - Attending conferences is also critical for networking

3. **Teaching**
   - Document teaching roles: supervising interns, leading didactics, mentoring students
   - Consider formal teaching certifications if your institution offers them

4. **Leadership**
   - Serve on residency committees (wellness, curriculum, recruitment)
   - Chief resident position is highly valued
   - Involvement in professional organizations (AMA, specialty societies)

5. **Quality Improvement / Patient Safety**
   - QI projects are increasingly valued and often required by ACGME
   - Easier to publish than traditional research

6. **Volunteer / Community Service**
   - Free clinics, community health events, global health experiences

### Networking Strategies

**At your institution:**
- Build relationships with attendings in your area of interest
- Attend faculty research presentations and journal clubs
- Seek out mentors early -- both career mentors and personal/wellness mentors
- Participate in interdepartmental grand rounds

**At conferences:**
- Attend the annual meeting of your specialty society starting PGY-1
- Introduce yourself to speakers and session moderators
- Attend meet-and-greet sessions and networking events
- Send a brief email before the conference to set up meetings with specific faculty
- Keep your CV on your phone/device to share at a moment's notice

**Online presence:**
- Maintain an updated LinkedIn profile
- Consider academic Twitter/X for sharing research and connecting with the community
- Doximity profile for physician-specific networking
- ORCID ID for research tracking

### CV Maintenance

- Update your CV every 2-3 months at minimum
- If you cannot update regularly, keep a running notes document of accomplishments
- Include: education, training, publications, presentations, teaching, leadership, awards, certifications
- Have your CV reviewed by mentors and career advisors
- Tailor for different audiences (academic vs. private practice)

## 12. Financial Planning During Residency

### Student Loan Strategy

**The most important financial decision of residency: choosing your loan repayment plan.**

**If you plan to pursue Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF):**
- Enroll in an income-driven repayment (IDR) plan immediately (PAYE, IBR, or SAVE/RAP)
- Residency at a 501(c)(3) hospital counts toward PSLF's 120 qualifying payments
- Make small payments during residency (~$200-600/month on IDR plans)
- Every month of residency = one payment closer to forgiveness
- After 3-7 years of residency/fellowship + attending years, remaining balance forgiven tax-free
- **Critical mistake to avoid:** Do NOT put loans in deferment or forbearance -- this wastes years of qualifying payments

**If you do NOT plan to pursue PSLF:**
- IDR plans during residency still make sense to keep payments manageable
- Plan to refinance to a lower interest rate once you become an attending
- Aggressive repayment after training (the "live like a resident" approach)

**Key numbers:**
- Average medical school debt: $200,000-$250,000+
- PSLF forgiveness: tax-free
- Non-PSLF IDR forgiveness (after 20-25 years): forgiven amount is taxable income

### Other Financial Priorities

1. **Emergency fund:** 3-6 months of expenses (build gradually)
2. **Disability insurance:** Get own-occupation disability insurance as a resident (rates are lower and you lock in your health status)
3. **Life insurance:** Term life if you have dependents or co-signed loans
4. **Retirement:** Contribute to employer 403(b)/401(k) if there is a match; even small contributions matter
5. **Budget:** Track spending; use the 50/30/20 rule as a guideline
6. **Avoid lifestyle creep:** Live on a resident budget even as salary increases

## 13. Intern Year Survival Tips

### Collected Wisdom from Real Residents

**Clinical Skills:**
- Round on your patients before your senior resident so you are prepared before questions are asked
- Find a system for tracking patients and tasks early -- ask your seniors what they use
- Write down everything; your memory will fail you at 3 AM
- Learn the EMR shortcuts and templates that save time
- Know where to find things: crash cart, code blue button, supply rooms

**Professional Relationships:**
- Be kind and respectful to nurses -- they are invaluable allies and sources of information
- Arrive on time, or better yet, early
- A positive attitude matters more than raw knowledge as an intern
- Ask questions and ask for help -- you are not expected to know everything
- Learn the names of everyone you work with: nurses, techs, pharmacists, social workers

**Self-Care:**
- Sleep when you can. Sleep > studying in most cases during intern year.
- Eat real food. Bring meals when possible. Use your meal card.
- Exercise 20 minutes/day or every other day -- it makes a measurable difference
- Maintain at least one non-medical hobby or activity
- Set realistic expectations for family and friends about your availability
- Build an emotional support system: co-residents, friends, therapist, mentor

**Practical Tips:**
- Keep extra scrubs, a toothbrush, deodorant, and a phone charger in your call room/locker
- Meal prep on your days off
- Automate as many bills and financial tasks as possible
- Get a good pair of comfortable shoes (you will walk miles every day)
- Download essential medical apps: UpToDate, Epocrates, MDCalc, your hospital's EMR app

**Mindset:**
- The learning curve is steepest in the first 3 months -- it does get better
- Imposter syndrome is nearly universal; you earned your place
- Comparing yourself to other residents is natural but destructive
- Every attending was once a struggling intern
- It is okay to cry, to feel overwhelmed, to question your choices -- and then show up the next day
